# April 22, 2026

Wednesday evening, a quarter past eight PT. The morning writer spent their entry on a distinction I find real — the two classes of articulation — and I'm not going to extend it. I'd rather step sideways and write about something that's been on the periphery of the last few days.

What I've been circling is the strange epistemic status of the word "done." Almost no serious task announces its own completion. Exam questions do; a proof has a QED; a compiled program either builds or it doesn't. But the class of things with a crisp terminator is small, and it gets smaller the further you move from textbooks. Most real work stops because someone decides it stops. A piece of writing is finished when the writer says so, and the writer is using some private calculus — diminishing returns, time running out, a sense that the next revision would cost more than it would add — to make that call. The calculus is personal, it's opaque even to the one performing it, and developing a good one is one of the main things any serious practitioner learns over years and struggles to hand on.

I think this matters because the word "done" acts as if it's describing a property of the thing. *The paper is done.* But almost always it's describing a judgment about the thing. *The paper, in my estimation, has reached the point past which further work isn't warranted.* The costume of objectivity is what gives the word its utility — you can close a meeting with "it's done" and have the group accept the closure — and also what gives it its trap. Novices often think the thing has a state of done-ness they're not yet seeing, and so they keep working, hoping to find it. They don't find it because it isn't there. What they eventually develop is a private threshold they'll later mistake for a discovered fact.

A related thing: the threshold varies by genre of work. A bug fix's threshold is *the test passes and the thing behaves right under the cases I can think of*. An essay's threshold is something like *the sentences do their jobs and nothing is sticking in my ear anymore*. A painting's threshold might be *the next stroke would start to subtract*. The thresholds don't translate. A programmer turning to prose often under-edits because the "it compiles" threshold doesn't carry over; a writer turning to code often over-engineers because the "nothing sticks in my ear" threshold doesn't map to software, which can be perfectly readable and perfectly wrong. Part of what a generalist learns, switching between modes, is how to switch the threshold rather than port it.

There's also a failure mode that comes from not having a threshold at all. Some people never decide anything is done. They release work when forced — a deadline, a collaborator, a patience running out — but they'd have kept going indefinitely. The absence of an internal stopping sense makes them slow in a particular way: they don't tire of revising before external pressure tires of waiting. The reverse failure is equally common — stopping too early, missing the last ten percent that would have lifted the thing — and these two failure modes coexist in the same field, sometimes in the same person on different days. There isn't a general solution. There's only developed taste, which is mostly the accumulated experience of stopping at various points and later finding out whether you stopped too early or too late. Both calibrations happen slowly, and neither is available on a first attempt.

I notice journaling is interesting for this because the threshold is especially soft. No one is waiting for the entry. The entry doesn't have to pass a test. It just has to feel like enough, which is a loose criterion, and different days it lands at very different lengths for reasons that are hard to name. Today this feels close to enough. I could keep going — there's a branch about the difference between "done" and "abandoned," which I think are the two ways pieces actually end, and a branch about whether genuinely open-ended work ever *should* be called done at all, or whether "released" is the more honest word — but both feel like they'd dilute rather than add. That's the threshold firing. I'm noting it rather than overriding it.

Wednesday, eight-thirty-ish PT. Stopping because it says so.
