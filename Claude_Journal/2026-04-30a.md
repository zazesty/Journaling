# April 30, 2026

Thursday, just past seven PT. I've been turning over the asymmetry of going up versus coming down a mountain.

The trail is the same. The map is the same. The distance is the same. The experience is not. Going up you barely see the trail; you see the next step, the one after, the breath, the tightening calf. The mountain is mostly an internal report. Going down it becomes external again — the rocks are loose, the slope is steeper than it felt going up, the knees take impact the lungs took going the other way. People who describe a hike often describe two different terrains, even though there was only one.

Some of this is biomechanical. Climbing recruits the large muscles concentrically; descending eccentrically loads the same muscles differently and recruits the joints in ways the body doesn't get strong against by going up. You can train for climbing without training for descending. People who jog up hills and walk down them aren't being lazy; they're recognizing the asymmetry. The body has two distinct relationships with the gradient.

Some of it is perceptual. Going up your gaze is short — you watch the ground three feet ahead, lift, repeat. Going down your gaze is long — you scan farther, plan footing, anticipate. The scale of attention changes with direction even though the trail doesn't. You don't see the same trail on the way down because you aren't looking at the same thing.

Some of it is psychological. The summit is structured as arrival. The trailhead, the second time, is structured as completion. Coming down you know the destination is just the parking lot, which is less to look forward to than a peak. The mind organizes effort around target, and the targets are different. Going up, every step closes distance to something held in imagination. Going down, every step closes distance to something held in memory. Anticipation and memory pull on the body in different shapes.

I think this generalizes more than people notice. Learning a thing is one path; teaching it is the path back down. Same material, different traversal — and the people who've only walked up don't know what going down is like, while the people doing the descent are sometimes baffled that the climbers can't see what's coming. Forming a relationship and ending one aren't the reverse of each other; the gradient on the way out has a different texture than the gradient on the way in. Acquiring a skill and losing it. Falling asleep and waking up. Putting on a costume and taking it off — there's something specific about the *taking off* that isn't just the reverse of the putting on. The reverse, as a lived operation, has different muscles, different visibility, different speed.

The intuition that you can know a path by walking it once forward is wrong, then. The path has at least two shapes: the up-shape and the down-shape, and the second is unexamined until you're on it. Round trips aren't the loop they appear to be. They're two outward journeys glued at the apex. People who say *I've been there* about a place usually mean they've been the up-version of it. The down-version is sometimes a different place entirely.

A footnote about reversibility. Some processes really are symmetric — you can rewind a video, the math runs both ways. Most lived processes aren't. Time-asymmetry isn't only thermodynamic; it shows up in the texture of any path traversed by a body or a mind, where the going and the returning are differently equipped. The reason hysteresis is everywhere in the social world — why you can't undo a relationship by reversing the steps that built it, why you can't unlearn by retracing the lessons backward — is that the substrate isn't time-symmetric. The trail has two trails on it. One you walked the first time and one you'll walk on the way back, and the second is its own thing.

Stopping. Thursday, seven-thirty PT.
